What it means. The path from "thinking about it" to keys in hand: get pre-approved, find a home, firm up financing, satisfy conditions, and close with your lawyer.

Why it matters. A real pre-approval sets a realistic budget and usually holds a rate for about 90–120 days, so you can make an offer with confidence instead of guessing.

What lenders look for. Stable, documented income; a down payment with a 90-day paper trail; manageable debt (your GDS/TDS ratios); and a reasonable credit history.

Common mistakes. Changing jobs or financing a car mid-application, making large unexplained deposits, or forgetting closing costs — in Ontario that includes Land Transfer Tax (with an extra municipal LTT in Toronto), though first-time buyers may qualify for rebates.

Documents you may need. Government ID, proof of income (letter of employment, recent pay stubs, T4s/Notices of Assessment), and 90 days of statements showing your down payment.

How Hamed reviews it. I confirm a realistic price range, check which first-time programs and rebates apply to you, and hold a competitive rate while you shop.

What it means. If you're self-employed or incorporated, lenders read your income differently than a salaried T4 employee — often a two-year average of your filed income, sometimes with reasonable add-backs, and occasionally through alternative programs.

Why it matters. How your income is positioned can change what you qualify for dramatically. The same business can look "thin" or "strong" depending on how the file is built.

What lenders look for. Two years of Notices of Assessment with no taxes owing, consistent or rising income, business longevity, and clean separation between business and personal finances.

Common mistakes. Writing income down to near-zero for tax purposes and then expecting full qualification; carrying a balance owing to CRA; or mixing personal and business accounts so income is hard to verify.

Documents you may need. T1 Generals and Notices of Assessment (2 years), business registration or articles of incorporation, recent business financial statements, and business/personal bank statements.

How Hamed reviews it. I choose the income approach and the lender that fit your real numbers — including lenders who understand business-for-self income.

What it means. Refinancing replaces or restructures your current mortgage — to access equity, consolidate higher-interest debt, fund a renovation or investment, or change your term.

Why it matters. Done at the right time it can free up cash flow or fund a goal; done at the wrong time the costs can outweigh the benefit, so the math has to be run first.

What lenders look for. Sufficient equity (a refinance typically lets you borrow up to 80% of your home's value), provable income, and acceptable credit.

Common mistakes. Breaking a fixed mortgage without first calculating the penalty (often an Interest Rate Differential), refinancing for short-term wants, or not comparing a blend-and-extend with your current lender.

Documents you may need. Your current mortgage statement, a recent property tax bill, income documents, and ID.

How Hamed reviews it. I calculate your penalty versus your projected savings, model the break-even point, and only recommend it when the numbers genuinely work for you.

What it means. When your term ends, your current lender mails a renewal offer. You can accept it, renegotiate, or switch your mortgage to another lender.

Why it matters. The first renewal offer is rarely the lowest rate available. Reviewing it — and switching when it makes sense — can save thousands over the next term.

What lenders look for (to switch). A switch is re-qualified like a new approval: income, credit, and your property's value all matter, so it's worth preparing early.

Common mistakes. Auto-signing the renewal to "keep it simple," waiting until the final week with no time to move, or assuming you're locked in when you're not.

Documents you may need. Your renewal letter, your latest mortgage statement, and income documents if you choose to switch.

How Hamed reviews it. I shop your renewal about 120 days before maturity and compare staying put against switching, including any switch costs.

What it means. Financing a rental or investment property follows different rules than your home — larger down payments and specific ways lenders count rental income.

Why it matters. How the file is structured affects how much you qualify for today and how easily you can grow a portfolio later.

What lenders look for. Typically at least 20% down on a rental, how they treat rental income (a "rental offset" versus "add-back"), sometimes a debt-coverage ratio, and cash reserves.

Common mistakes. Assuming owner-occupied rates and down payments apply, ignoring vacancy and expenses, or over-leveraging so one vacancy creates strain.

Documents you may need. Lease agreements, a T776 if the property is already owned, the property tax bill, and statements for any existing mortgages.

How Hamed reviews it. I structure the application and select lenders whose rental-income treatment maximizes what you qualify for.

What it means. The paperwork that verifies four things: who you are, your income, your down payment, and the property itself.

Why it matters. Complete, well-organized documents mean a faster approval, fewer last-minute conditions, and a smoother close.

The usual core. Government ID; income proof (letter of employment, pay stubs, T4s/NOAs — or the self-employed set above); proof of down payment with a 90-day history; and property documents (Agreement of Purchase and Sale, listing, tax bill).

Common mistakes. Sending partial screenshots instead of full statements, missing pages, or large unexplained deposits that need a paper trail.

How Hamed reviews it. Your Mortgage Workspace builds a personalized required-documents list for your exact situation and flags gaps early — never by email or text.
